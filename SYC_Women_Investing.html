Why This Section Exists

Women are statistically less likely to invest than men — and that gap has a significant long-term cost. Not because of ability or interest, but because of a financial services industry that has historically failed to speak to women in a relevant, accessible way.

Stack Your Cash exists to change that. This section brings together all of our content specifically relevant to women investors — from first steps to advanced strategy — in one place. No condescension, no pink-washing. Just plain-English investing knowledge that works.

The Opportunity

When women invest, they tend to invest well — with longer time horizons, lower turnover, and better risk-adjusted returns on average. The problem isn't capability. It's access, confidence, and relevance of information. That's exactly what this section aims to address.

Jargon Buster

New to investing or just want a quick reminder? Here are the key terms that come up most in investing conversations — explained in plain English.

ISA (Individual Savings Account)
A tax-free wrapper for your savings or investments. Up to £20,000 per year — any growth or income inside is free from UK tax.
ETF (Exchange-Traded Fund)
A fund that holds hundreds of companies in one go — giving instant diversification. Bought and sold like a share on the stock exchange.
Compound Growth
Earning returns on your returns. A small regular investment, left to grow over decades, can become significantly larger than the total you put in.
Risk Tolerance
How comfortable you are with your investments going down in value short-term. Higher risk can mean higher long-term returns — but requires patience.
Diversification
Spreading your money across different investments so no single one can sink your whole portfolio. "Don't put all your eggs in one basket."
Pension (SIPP)
A Self-Invested Personal Pension — lets you choose your own investments inside a pension wrapper with tax relief added by the government.
Pound-Cost Averaging
Investing a fixed amount regularly regardless of price. Automatically buys more when prices are lower — takes the stress out of market timing.
OCF (Ongoing Charges Figure)
The annual cost of owning a fund, expressed as a percentage. A world tracker ETF typically costs 0.1–0.25% per year — very low.
